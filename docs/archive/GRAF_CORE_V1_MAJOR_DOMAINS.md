# GRAF CORE v1 — First 5 Major Domains

## SECTION 1 — DOMAIN SELECTION THESIS

A domain is worthy of v1 only if it behaves like a **load-bearing territory** in the graph, not a static category. For GRAF, that means the domain must do five things immediately: (1) generate meaningful nodes/edges through normal use, (2) create measurable pressure and tradeoffs, (3) expose route logic (what to do next), (4) support at least 2–3 concrete embedded tools, and (5) interact with other domains in ways that produce non-obvious insights.

### Selection criteria used
1. **Universality**: Relevant to the majority of users regardless of profession, age, or context.
2. **Graph productivity**: Naturally creates relationships, dependencies, and feedback loops.
3. **Intervention readiness**: Can host tools that change behavior quickly (not just describe it).
4. **Signal quality**: Can produce support signals that are collectable in v1 without excessive instrumentation.
5. **Compounding value**: Improvements in the domain should unlock gains in multiple other domains.
6. **Failure clarity**: The domain has recognizable degradation modes that can trigger alerts/routes.

### Weaker candidates rejected/postponed
- **Identity/Meaning**: High value but often too abstract early; too easy to become journaling theater without behavioral anchors.
- **Community/Relationships (broad)**: Critical long-term, but v1 major status risks privacy complexity and noisy graph data. Keep targeted relational support signals first.
- **Learning/Knowledge**: Powerful but often derivative of Energy + Execution + Direction. Better as subdomain under Craft/Capability initially.
- **Finance/Resources as major**: Important but too narrow for some users; strong as subdomain inside Stability.
- **Creativity/Expression as major**: High upside but less universal than operational fundamentals in first release.

### Why these 5 are the strongest foundation
The selected five domains form a closed operational loop: **Energy & State → Stability & Constraints → Direction & Commitments → Execution & Delivery → Capability & Craft**, then back into energy/state through load and recovery. This creates immediate route intelligence, realistic forecasting, and credible intervention points without bloating ontology or UX.

## SECTION 2 — THE 5 MAJOR DOMAINS

## 1) Energy & State
- **Plain-language meaning**: Your functional capacity right now and over time (sleep, focus, mood regulation, physiological readiness).
- **Why it matters structurally**: It is the primary throughput gate for every other domain.
- **Leverage created**: Small improvements multiply outcomes in execution, learning speed, and relational quality.
- **Pressure/risk created**: Misread state causes overcommitment, collapse cycles, and false self-assessment.
- **Strongest interactions**: Stability & Constraints, Execution & Delivery.
- **Why major (not minor)**: No other domain has stronger system-wide causal effect per unit change.

## 2) Stability & Constraints
- **Plain-language meaning**: The condition of your life infrastructure (time, money runway, obligations, admin load, environment reliability).
- **Why it matters structurally**: Defines feasible action space and survivability under stress.
- **Leverage created**: Reduces friction tax; frees bandwidth for execution and strategic work.
- **Pressure/risk created**: Hidden constraints cause chronic route failure despite good intentions.
- **Strongest interactions**: Direction & Commitments, Execution & Delivery.
- **Why major**: It determines whether plans are physically executable in real life.

## 3) Direction & Commitments
- **Plain-language meaning**: What matters now, what you are committed to, and what you are deliberately not doing.
- **Why it matters structurally**: Provides graph-level prioritization and conflict resolution.
- **Leverage created**: Converts effort from scattered activity into coherent momentum.
- **Pressure/risk created**: Ambiguous commitments create drift, overexpansion, and guilt loops.
- **Strongest interactions**: Execution & Delivery, Capability & Craft.
- **Why major**: Without direction, other domains optimize locally and sabotage globally.

## 4) Execution & Delivery
- **Plain-language meaning**: Turning commitments into completed outcomes through sequencing, cadence, and closure.
- **Why it matters structurally**: Produces objective proof of progress and route validity.
- **Leverage created**: Builds trust loops, momentum, and practical confidence.
- **Pressure/risk created**: Activity theater (busy but not effective); burnout via unsustainable cadence.
- **Strongest interactions**: Energy & State, Direction & Commitments.
- **Why major**: It is where graph intelligence is tested against reality.

## 5) Capability & Craft
- **Plain-language meaning**: The skills, methods, and quality standards that determine how well you can perform key work.
- **Why it matters structurally**: Sets quality ceiling and adaptation rate.
- **Leverage created**: Higher capability compresses effort and increases strategic options.
- **Pressure/risk created**: Skill debt leads to repeated bottlenecks and fragile performance.
- **Strongest interactions**: Execution & Delivery, Direction & Commitments.
- **Why major**: Sustainable improvement requires growing the operator, not only managing tasks.

## SECTION 3 — INTERNAL STRUCTURE OF EACH DOMAIN

## 1) Energy & State
- **Key subdomains**: Sleep/recovery, cognitive clarity, emotional regulation, somatic load, attention stability.
- **Core node types**: State snapshot, recovery protocol, trigger pattern, depletion event, regulation tactic.
- **Support signals**: Sleep duration/regularity, subjective energy score, interruption count, recovery compliance, stress spikes.
- **Shallow vs deep**:
  - Shallow: Occasional mood logging with no behavior link.
  - Deep: State-to-output correlations and adaptive planning based on state trends.
- **Mature when**: User can predict bad days early and switch protocols before failure.
- **Forecast-ready when**: State trends reliably anticipate execution capacity by day/week.
- **Pseudo-deep when**: Excessive self-tracking with no route or scheduling changes.

## 2) Stability & Constraints
- **Key subdomains**: Time architecture, financial runway, obligations ledger, admin overhead, environment reliability.
- **Core node types**: Constraint, buffer, obligation, friction source, failure mode.
- **Support signals**: Open obligations count, calendar fragmentation, runway weeks, unresolved admin tasks, context-switch rate.
- **Shallow vs deep**:
  - Shallow: To-do dumping without constraint modeling.
  - Deep: Explicit constraint graph with mitigation routes and buffers.
- **Mature when**: Surprises decrease and weekly plan viability improves.
- **Forecast-ready when**: Constraint pressure predicts schedule slippage and recovery needs.
- **Pseudo-deep when**: Color-coded systems that hide unresolved hard limits.

## 3) Direction & Commitments
- **Key subdomains**: Active horizons, explicit commitments, anti-goals, decision principles, tradeoff policy.
- **Core node types**: Priority node, commitment contract, anti-commitment, decision rule, scope boundary.
- **Support signals**: Commitment count, priority churn, decision latency, active-goal spread, abandonment rate.
- **Shallow vs deep**:
  - Shallow: Inspirational goals with no sacrifice logic.
  - Deep: Explicit yes/no boundaries and measurable commitment windows.
- **Mature when**: User can explain what is deferred and why without ambiguity.
- **Forecast-ready when**: Commitment load predicts delivery capacity and conflict risk.
- **Pseudo-deep when**: Vision statements not tied to scheduling or resource decisions.

## 4) Execution & Delivery
- **Key subdomains**: Work breakdown, sequencing, cadence, closure quality, review loops.
- **Core node types**: Deliverable, step node, blocker, handoff edge, completion proof.
- **Support signals**: Weekly completion rate, carryover ratio, blocker age, cycle time, closure quality score.
- **Shallow vs deep**:
  - Shallow: Long task lists and constant reprioritization.
  - Deep: Stable execution rhythm with reliable closure and post-mortem learning.
- **Mature when**: Completion is predictable and blockers are surfaced early.
- **Forecast-ready when**: Throughput trends estimate delivery dates with acceptable confidence.
- **Pseudo-deep when**: Productivity rituals increase activity but not shipped outcomes.

## 5) Capability & Craft
- **Key subdomains**: Core skills, methods/tooling, quality heuristics, deliberate practice, feedback integration.
- **Core node types**: Skill asset, method pattern, quality rubric, practice cycle, bottleneck diagnosis.
- **Support signals**: Error recurrence rate, rework time, practice consistency, mentor/peer feedback delta, quality drift.
- **Shallow vs deep**:
  - Shallow: Consuming content without skill transfer.
  - Deep: Repeated practice tied to specific performance bottlenecks.
- **Mature when**: User can deliberately improve a weak capability with measurable impact.
- **Forecast-ready when**: Capability trend predicts reduced cycle time and higher quality output.
- **Pseudo-deep when**: Endless learning artifacts disconnected from production work.

## SECTION 4 — EMBEDDED TOOLS BY DOMAIN

## 1) Energy & State tools
1. **State-to-Load Allocator (v1)**
   - Does: Recommends daily workload tier (heavy/medium/light/recovery).
   - Lives: Domain layer.
   - Inputs: Recent sleep, current energy rating, prior-day strain, hard deadlines.
   - Output: Load budget + suggested task types.
   - Graph change: Adds/updates edges from state nodes to execution queue nodes.
2. **Crash Pattern Detector (v1)**
   - Does: Detects recurring depletion sequences.
   - Lives: Edge analytics between state snapshots and missed commitments.
   - Inputs: Time series of state + failure events.
   - Output: Pattern alert + preventive protocol suggestion.
   - Graph change: Creates risk edges and protocol nodes.
3. **Advanced biometric integration (later)**
   - Does: Ingests wearables for finer state predictions.
   - Lives: Domain signal pipeline.
   - Inputs: External biometric APIs.
   - Output: Refined readiness score.
   - Graph change: Increases signal confidence weights.

## 2) Stability & Constraints tools
1. **Constraint Mapper (v1)**
   - Does: Converts obligations/resources into explicit constraints.
   - Lives: Domain layer.
   - Inputs: Calendar, recurring obligations, budget/runway, fixed responsibilities.
   - Output: Constraint map + available capacity windows.
   - Graph change: Adds hard-boundary nodes and feasibility edges.
2. **Buffer Health Gauge (v1)**
   - Does: Monitors time/finance/attention buffers.
   - Lives: Node-level tool on buffer nodes.
   - Inputs: Weekly buffer consumption and replenishment data.
   - Output: Buffer status (stable/eroding/critical).
   - Graph change: Flags risk escalation routes.
3. **Scenario Shock Simulator (later)**
   - Does: Simulates disruptions (expense spike, illness week, deadline compression).
   - Lives: Domain simulation layer.
   - Inputs: Baseline constraints + hypothetical shocks.
   - Output: Survival and adaptation scenarios.
   - Graph change: Creates contingency route templates.

## 3) Direction & Commitments tools
1. **Commitment Contract Builder (v1)**
   - Does: Forces commitment definition with scope, deadline, tradeoff, and success proof.
   - Lives: Node template for commitment nodes.
   - Inputs: Target outcome, time window, sacrifice list.
   - Output: Structured commitment object.
   - Graph change: Creates explicit commitment edges to execution and constraints.
2. **Conflict Resolver (v1)**
   - Does: Detects mutually incompatible commitments.
   - Lives: Edge-level analysis across commitment nodes.
   - Inputs: Commitment load, available capacity, deadlines.
   - Output: Ranked conflicts + recommended drop/defer decisions.
   - Graph change: Adds conflict edges and decision checkpoints.
3. **Horizon Portfolio Optimizer (later)**
   - Does: Balances short vs medium horizon commitments.
   - Lives: Domain planning layer.
   - Inputs: Outcome value estimates + risk/effort.
   - Output: Portfolio recommendation.
   - Graph change: Reweights priority edges across horizons.

## 4) Execution & Delivery tools
1. **Route Builder (v1)**
   - Does: Converts a commitment into minimal viable route steps.
   - Lives: Node-to-subgraph tool.
   - Inputs: Commitment contract + known constraints.
   - Output: Step graph with sequence and dependencies.
   - Graph change: Creates actionable route subgraph.
2. **Blocker Escalation Engine (v1)**
   - Does: Detects stalled nodes and triggers escalation options.
   - Lives: Edge/node health monitor.
   - Inputs: Blocker age, dependency status, owner signals.
   - Output: Escalation action list (delegate, rescope, defer, remove).
   - Graph change: Inserts intervention nodes and revised edges.
3. **Delivery Forecast Panel (v1)**
   - Does: Estimates probability of completion on current route.
   - Lives: Domain layer analytics.
   - Inputs: Throughput trend, open blockers, capacity forecast.
   - Output: Confidence band + projected delivery window.
   - Graph change: Updates route confidence weights.

## 5) Capability & Craft tools
1. **Bottleneck Diagnosis (v1)**
   - Does: Links repeated execution failures to skill/method gaps.
   - Lives: Cross-domain edge analysis (execution ↔ capability).
   - Inputs: Rework logs, blocker taxonomy, quality failures.
   - Output: Ranked capability gaps.
   - Graph change: Creates capability debt edges.
2. **Practice Loop Planner (v1)**
   - Does: Turns a capability gap into a short deliberate-practice cycle.
   - Lives: Node template for skill improvement routes.
   - Inputs: Target skill, available time, context constraints.
   - Output: Practice plan with checkpoints.
   - Graph change: Adds practice nodes tied to delivery routes.
3. **Quality Drift Monitor (later)**
   - Does: Detects decline in output quality over time.
   - Lives: Domain analytics layer.
   - Inputs: QA/feedback signals and rework rates.
   - Output: Drift alert + corrective method suggestion.
   - Graph change: Adjusts confidence and risk edges for affected work types.

## SECTION 5 — CROSS-DOMAIN INTERACTION MODEL

### Strongest relationships
1. **Energy & State ↔ Execution & Delivery**: State quality directly modulates throughput and blocker resolution speed.
2. **Stability & Constraints ↔ Direction & Commitments**: Commitments are only valid if constraints permit them.
3. **Execution & Delivery ↔ Capability & Craft**: Repeated blockers reveal skill debt; skill gains improve delivery reliability.

### Common support patterns
- Better stability buffers protect energy during peak delivery periods.
- Clear direction reduces execution thrash and preserves cognitive load.
- Capability growth lowers constraint pressure by increasing efficiency.

### Common destabilization patterns
- Direction inflation (too many commitments) destabilizes energy and execution.
- Poor stability (admin chaos, low runway) destabilizes all forecasts.
- Over-indexing on capability building can hide delivery avoidance.

### Common masking patterns
- High execution activity can mask weak direction.
- Strong motivation spikes can temporarily mask poor stability.
- Learning consumption can mask low real capability transfer.

### Delayed effects
- Capability investment often yields delayed but compounding execution gains.
- Stability erosion often appears late as sudden delivery collapse.
- Energy debt can lag 1–2 weeks before visible output failure.

### Useful cross-domain links for v1
- State-to-load edges.
- Constraint-to-commitment feasibility edges.
- Commitment conflict edges.
- Execution failure to capability-gap edges.

### Links to wait until later
- Full social graph impacts (team/family multi-agent effects).
- Long-horizon identity narrative modeling.
- External market/opportunity probabilistic modeling.

## SECTION 6 — STARTING PRIORITY AND ENTRY LOGIC

- **Should user choose one central domain first?** Yes: user picks a current pain center from 3 prompts: “I can’t focus”, “I can’t fit everything”, “I’m not finishing what matters.”
- **Should system suggest a domain first?** Yes: quick intake suggests a likely primary domain with confidence score and allows override.
- **What makes strong first entry?** High pain + high controllability + immediate measurable improvement.
- **Should all 5 be open immediately?** Visible, yes; fully interactive, no.
- **Partially locked domains?** Yes. Non-primary domains open in lite mode (signals + key links) until baseline in primary domain is established.
- **How first domain opens second/third**:
  - If primary is Energy, system opens Execution lite and Stability lite next.
  - If primary is Stability, system opens Direction next to prune impossible commitments.
  - If primary is Execution, system opens Direction and Energy immediately to prevent activity traps.

This yields a real Start experience: one anchor domain, two adjacent domains, and immediate edge formation rather than broad unfocused setup.

## SECTION 7 — DEPTH / UNLOCK MODEL BY DOMAIN

## 1) Energy & State
- **Basic**: Daily state check + load tier suggestion.
- **Expanded**: Pattern detection + recovery protocol library.
- **Advanced**: Predictive state trend and route adaptation.
- **Expert**: Multi-factor resilience planning (later, optional).
- **Unlocks happen via**: 2+ weeks consistent signal capture.
- **Gains**: Fewer crashes, better day-level planning.
- **Protect from premature opening**: Complex biometrics and overly granular tracking.

## 2) Stability & Constraints
- **Basic**: Constraint inventory and weekly capacity estimate.
- **Expanded**: Buffer tracking and obligation risk alerts.
- **Advanced**: Contingency routes and scenario simulations.
- **Expert**: Dynamic resource reallocation modeling (later).
- **Unlocks via**: Completion of baseline obligation graph + one weekly review cycle.
- **Gains**: Higher plan realism and lower surprise load.
- **Protect**: Overly detailed finance/admin modeling before core clarity.

## 3) Direction & Commitments
- **Basic**: 1–3 active commitments with explicit no-list.
- **Expanded**: Conflict detection and horizon balancing.
- **Advanced**: Commitment portfolio calibration by capacity and value.
- **Expert**: Strategy-level tradeoff simulations (later).
- **Unlocks via**: Demonstrated closure on initial commitments.
- **Gains**: Less drift, clearer tradeoffs.
- **Protect**: Ambitious multi-horizon planning without execution evidence.

## 4) Execution & Delivery
- **Basic**: Route builder + weekly completion tracking.
- **Expanded**: Blocker escalation and closure quality scoring.
- **Advanced**: Delivery probability forecasting.
- **Expert**: Automated route re-planning under dynamic constraints.
- **Unlocks via**: Stable cadence data across 2–4 weeks.
- **Gains**: Reliable shipping rhythm and better predictions.
- **Protect**: Premature optimization dashboards without enough data.

## 5) Capability & Craft
- **Basic**: Identify top capability bottleneck and one practice loop.
- **Expanded**: Multiple skill loops tied to active commitments.
- **Advanced**: Quality drift detection and method portfolio tuning.
- **Expert**: Personalized capability compounding strategies.
- **Unlocks via**: Repeated evidence that capability gap causes delivery friction.
- **Gains**: Better quality at lower effort.
- **Protect**: Learning sprawl disconnected from real work.

   - Dependency created: Provides outcome data needed by all other domains.

2. **Direction & Commitments (build second)**
   - Why now: Prevents execution from becoming busywork.
   - Value unlocked: Priority clarity and conflict handling.
   - Dependency created: Defines what execution routes should optimize for.

3. **Energy & State (build third)**
   - Why now: Enables adaptive planning and reduces failure volatility.
   - Value unlocked: Better capacity matching and collapse prevention.
   - Dependency created: Feeds execution forecast quality.

4. **Stability & Constraints (build fourth)**
   - Why now: Improves realism once users see recurring feasibility issues.
   - Value unlocked: Practical survivability and reduced hidden load.
   - Dependency created: Hard boundaries for reliable planning.

5. **Capability & Craft (build fifth)**
   - Why now: Most valuable after execution data reveals bottlenecks.

   - Dependency created: Converts recurring failures into growth loops.

## SECTION 9 — WHAT TO POSTPONE

1. **Identity / Meaning System**
- Tempting because: Feels foundational and philosophically central.
- Should wait because: Easy to fake depth without behavior change.
- Later role: Major domain candidate after execution evidence exists.

2. **Broad Relationships / Social Graph**
- Tempting because: Human outcomes are highly social.
- Should wait because: Privacy/consent complexity and noisy attribution in v1.
- Later role: Major domain (multi-agent layer) in v2+.

3. **Finance as standalone major**
- Tempting because: High stress driver.
- Should wait because: Not universal in same way; best embedded under Stability initially.
- Later role: Could become major for specific user segments.

4. **Learning/Knowledge Management as standalone major**
- Tempting because: Popular productivity territory.
- Should wait because: Often an intermediate mechanism, not end-state leverage.
- Later role: Subdomain of Capability; possible major later for creator/knowledge-heavy cohorts.

5. **Creativity/Expression domain**
- Tempting because: Strong user identity pull.
- Should wait because: Lower universal necessity for base operating system reliability.
- Later role: Major for expanded personal OS editions.

## SECTION 10 — OUTPUT FORMAT

### A) Final 5-domain v1 set
1. Energy & State
2. Stability & Constraints
3. Direction & Commitments
4. Execution & Delivery
5. Capability & Craft

### B) Why this is the right foundation (one paragraph)
This set is the strongest v1 foundation because it captures the minimum complete operating loop of human performance: available capacity, real-world feasibility, chosen priorities, shipped outcomes, and adaptive skill growth. Together, these domains generate high-quality graph edges early, expose practical tradeoffs, support real embedded tools, and create both immediate utility (better weekly execution) and compounding intelligence (better forecasting and route calibration). It is broad enough to feel universally relevant, but narrow enough to remain buildable and behaviorally credible in v1.
